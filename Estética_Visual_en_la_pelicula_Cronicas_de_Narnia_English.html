ART

Visual art and design play a crucial role in bringing the magical world of Narnia to life. Below is some information about the art and design used in the film.

MAKE-UP


The makeup in The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe was instrumental in bringing the fantastical characters to life and creating the magical atmosphere of Narnia. The makeup design for Queen Jadis, played by Tilda Swinton, features cool tones and icy details that reflect her control over winter and her inhuman nature. Her appearance contributes to her image as a menacing and powerful figure. For the mythological creatures, such as fauns, centaurs and dwarves, specialized prosthetics and makeup techniques were employed that fuse the human with the animal, achieving a realistic effect. Mr. Tumnus, the faun, stands out with a tender and friendly appearance, allowing viewers to emotionally connect with him. Overall, the makeup in the film enriches the narrative and highlights the magic.




WHITE WITCH

Her makeup was characterized by pale, cool tones that reflected her control over winter and her inhuman nature. Icy details were added to her hair and face, contributing to her menacing image.

MYTHOLOGICAL CREATURES

Prosthetics and specialized makeup techniques were used for the centaurs, fauns, and dwarves, merging the human with the animal, achieving a realistic and convincing effect.

Mr. Tumnus

Specific makeup techniques were used to give him a tender and gentle appearance, allowing viewers to emotionally connect with his friendly nature.

PHOTOGRAPH


The cinematography in The Chronicles of Narnia: The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe is instrumental in creating the magical atmosphere and visual contrast between the real and fantastical worlds. Under the direction of photography of Donald McAlpine, each shot was carefully crafted to immerse viewers in the rich and vibrant universe of Narnia. The combination of visual effects and traditional photography enriches the visual narrative. Action shots, complemented by CGI, bring mythical creatures and magical environments to life, creating an immersive experience.
McAlpine's ability to balance these technical elements is key to transporting viewers to Narnia and making every moment memorable.

The cinematography highlights the contrast between the dark world of the Pevensies and the bright world of Narnia, highlighting the transformation of the environment.

SCENOGRAPHY

Cinematographer Donald McAlpine used a combination of techniques to capture the magical essence of Narnia. The cinematography focused on creating a contrast between the “real” world of the Pevensies (dark, earthy and muted) and the world of Narnia (white, bright and vibrant under the spell of winter). Anamorphic lenses were used to capture the vast landscapes of Narnia, giving it an epic and majestic scale. As winter fades and Narnia transforms, warm, golden tones take center stage, highlighting the rebirth of life.

PROPS

Props in the film included a combination of magical and medieval elements. Notable among them were the weapons used by the Pevensies in the final battle, such as Peter's sword, Susan's bow and arrows, and the shield. These objects were designed in great detail, inspired by medieval weaponry with fantastical touches. Also included was the White Witch's magic wand, which was created to resemble a piece of crystallized ice, capable of freezing her enemies. Objects in Mr. Tumnus' lair, such as the oil lamps and cups, were selected to give a cosy, rural feel, in contrast to the cold oppression of the rest of Narnia.

SCENARIO DESIGN

The set design for The Chronicles of Narnia was based on a mix of real and fantastical elements. The designers took inspiration from British landscapes and combined them with imaginary elements to create Narnia. The winter forest was one of the most iconic sets, with trees covered in artificial snow, designed to convey the vastness and solitude under Queen Jadis' rule. The Great Battlefield was a huge outdoor set, where elements for the action scenes were created, combining practical and digital effects. Other sets, such as the White Witch's throne and the spring landscapes that emerge at the end of the winter spell, were built in studios and were complemented with CGI effects to expand their visual scale.
